Fitness, Physicality and Movement, my passions and passtimes

written by Joseph Mothopeng-Ferris

Ever since I was a a child I have been fascinated by movement and physicality. From martial arts and dance to gymnastics and athletics, I have explored all sorts of different physical art forms, even contemporary mime, and whilst studying them all, I have noticed common elements that sit at each of their foundations.

Strength

Developing strength takes time, a long time. To develop it you must take yourself close to approx. 85% absolute intensity for the target tissue. In laymans terms that means taking yourself to a point near the physiological failure and rupturing of said tissue. In your bodies terms, this is a near death experience. You do this, and then rest to allow your body to reinforce those tisses so that you come back stronger. You do this for days, weeks, months, years and decades allowing for adaption in all parts of yourself, nerve, muscle, tendon, ligamens and bone all the while learning about yourself. Learning your tolerances and pains and what your bodily sensations mean. It also teaches you patience, diligence and mental fortitude as well as instilling the skill of self forgiveness for you will fail continously upwards. These qualities carry over into all manner of disciples across life and are the true boon of strength training.

Conditioning

Conditioning is the ability to express strength over long periods of time continuously. without conditioning expression of strength becomes very physiologically expensive and the recovery times long thus diminishing the utility of strength. Conditioning allows you to to perform acts of strength day after day with lower risk of injury or fatigue.

Mobility

Mobility is the raw quality of flexibility brought under control by the neuro-muscular system. instead of needing an external object or force to pull or push you into a shape your mind can simply will it to occur. It allows the most avenues of expression for strength and conditioning.

Coordination

Coordination is the glue that binds the other three qualities together, creating a synergy between them that ultimately leads to physical capacity worth far more than the sum of their individual parts. There are several key elements to coordination that include:

  • hand eye coordination - throwing, catching, juggling etc
  • whole body movements - running, jumping, climbing etc
  • balancing - single leg and on hands
